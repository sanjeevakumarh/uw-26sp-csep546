Problem **A1.** After your yearly checkup, the doctor has bad news and good news. The bad news is that you tested positive for a serious disease, and that the test is 99% accurate (i.e., the probability of testing positive given that you have the disease is 0.99, as is the probability of testing negative given that you don't have the disease). The good news is that this is a rare disease, striking only one in 10,000 people. What are the chances that you actually have the disease?

- Breaking the problem down for knowns and unknowns.
	- Known information
		- The disease is extremely rare (1 in 10,000), so 99% accurate test will produce ~101 positive tests, of which only 1 person has the disease, meaning 1 true positive and 100 false positives.
		- Therefore, total probability of testing positive comes from two groups: people with the disease who test positive + people without the disease who test positive.
	- Hypothesis => P(H) => you have the disease
		- Prior - P(H) = 1/10,000 = 0.0001 (rate of the disease in population)
		- P (E | H ) = 0.99 (true positive, test is positive given you have D)
		- P (E | ¬ H ) = 0.01 (false positive, test is positive given you dont have D )
		- First, compute P(E) — the probability of testing positive across the entire population.
			- which is sum of
				- people with disease testing positive => P(E|H)·P(H)
				- people with no disease testing positive => P(E|¬H)·P(¬H)
			- P(E) = P(E|H)·P(H) +P(E|¬H)·P(¬H)
			- P(E) = (0.99)(0.0001) + (0.01)(0.9999)
			- P(E) = 0.000099 + 0.009999 = 0.010098
		- Apply Bayes' theorem — of all the people who tested positive, what fraction truly have the disease
			- P(H|E) = P(E|H)·P(H) / P(E)
			- P(H|E) = (0.99)·(0.0001) / (0.010098)
			- P(H|E) = 0.000099 / 0.010098 = 0.00980392156
	- Final Answer ≈ 0.9804%
